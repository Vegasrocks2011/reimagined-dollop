Ecosystems — Food Chains, Food Webs, Factors & Pyramids

GCSE Biology · Ecosystems · Interdependence · Ecological Relationships

1 · Ecosystems & Habitats
Key definitions
EcosystemAll the living organisms in an area together with their non-living environment, and the interactions between them.
HabitatThe place where an organism lives. Provides food, shelter, water and space for reproduction.
PopulationAll the individuals of one species living in a habitat at the same time.
CommunityAll the populations of different species living and interacting in a habitat at the same time.
SpeciesA group of organisms that can breed together to produce fertile offspring.
NicheThe role of an organism in its ecosystem — what it eats, where it lives, when it is active, and how it interacts with other organisms.
BiodiversityThe variety of different species in an ecosystem. High biodiversity = more stable ecosystem.
AdaptationA feature of an organism that makes it better suited to survive and reproduce in its environment.
Ecosystem → Community → Population → Individual organism. These are the levels of ecological organisation from largest to smallest.
2 · Biotic & Abiotic Factors
Biotic factors

Living factors that affect organisms in an ecosystem.

PredationPredators eating prey — reduces prey population, affects predator numbers.
CompetitionOrganisms competing for the same limited resources (food, water, mates, territory, light).
DiseasePathogens reduce population size; can spread rapidly through dense populations.
ParasitismOne organism benefits (parasite) at the expense of another (host).
MutualismBoth organisms benefit from the relationship (e.g. cleaner fish and sharks).
Food availabilityAffects population size — fewer resources → smaller population.
Abiotic factors

Non-living (physical and chemical) factors that affect organisms.

Light intensityAffects rate of photosynthesis — essential for plants and algae.
TemperatureAffects enzyme activity, metabolic rates and distribution of species.
Water / rainfallEssential for life; affects distribution of plants and animals.
HumidityAffects water loss from organisms; important for transpiration.
Soil pH / mineralsAffects plant growth; different plants thrive at different pH levels.
Carbon dioxide levelAffects rate of photosynthesis in plants.
Wind speedAffects evaporation and transpiration; shapes plant growth.
Oxygen levelCritical for aquatic organisms; affects decomposition rates.
⚠ Biotic = Biological (living). Abiotic = Absence of life (non-living). A common exam error is confusing the two — always check whether the factor is a living organism or a physical/chemical condition.
3 · Food Chains
Food chains — key rules
ProducerAlways the start of the chain. Green plants / algae that make food by photosynthesis. Also called autotrophs.
Primary consumerEats the producer. Herbivore. First trophic level after producers.
Secondary consumerEats the primary consumer. Often a carnivore or omnivore.
Tertiary consumerEats the secondary consumer. Top predator in many chains.
DecomposerBacteria and fungi that break down dead organisms. Return nutrients to soil. Essential for cycling.
Arrow directionArrows show direction of energy flow, not "what eats what". Arrow points FROM food TO feeder: Grass → Rabbit → Fox.
Trophic levelThe position of an organism in the food chain. Producers = level 1; primary consumers = level 2; etc.
Energy transferOnly ~10% of energy is transferred between trophic levels. 90% is lost as heat, movement, excretion, and uneaten parts.
HerbivoreEats only plants. Carnivore: eats only animals. Omnivore: eats both.
Example food chain: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Hawk
Grass (producer) → Grasshopper (primary consumer) → Frog (secondary consumer) → Snake (tertiary consumer) → Hawk (quaternary consumer)
4 · Food Webs
What is a food web?

A food web shows all the feeding relationships in an ecosystem — it is a network of interconnected food chains. Most organisms eat more than one type of food and are eaten by more than one type of predator, so food webs are more realistic than single food chains.

Interpreting food webs

Removing a speciesCan cause a cascade — prey population rises; predator population falls. Knock-on effects throughout the web.
Adding a speciesMay outcompete existing species; alter population sizes throughout the web.
Increasing a speciesIts prey is eaten more → prey decreases; its predators have more food → increase.

Food web advantages over food chains

  • More realistic — shows multiple feeding relationships
  • Shows how a change in one population affects many others
  • Shows which species are keystone species (critical to web stability)
  • Illustrates competition between species at the same level
5 · Interdependence
Interdependence

All organisms in an ecosystem depend on each other in some way. Changes to one population cause knock-on effects throughout the entire community. This is called interdependence.

Types of relationships

Predator-preyPredator and prey populations are linked — as prey increases, predator increases; then prey decreases as predated more → predator falls → prey recovers. Cyclical relationship.
CompetitionIntraspecific (same species) or interspecific (different species) competition for food, water, space, mates or light.
MutualismBoth organisms benefit. Example: bees pollinate flowers; flowers provide nectar. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria in plant roots.
ParasitismParasite benefits; host is harmed. Tapeworms in intestines; fleas on mammals; mistletoe on trees.

Predator-prey cycle

1. Prey population increases → more food for predators

2. Predator population increases → more predation

3. Prey population decreases (overpredated)

4. Less food → predator population decreases

5. Less predation → prey population recovers → cycle repeats

On a graph: the predator curve follows (lags behind) the prey curve. Predator peaks slightly after prey peaks.
6 · Pyramids of Numbers
Pyramid of numbers

Shows the number of organisms at each trophic level in a food chain. Each bar represents a trophic level; the width represents the number of organisms at that level.

Advantages

  • Easy to construct — just count organisms
  • Shows feeding relationships clearly

Disadvantages

  • Does not account for organism size — one large tree vs millions of grass plants
  • Can be inverted (e.g. parasitic chains, or one large producer)
  • Does not show energy or biomass transferred
Pyramids of numbers can be inverted (wider at top) when one large producer supports many consumers, or in a parasitic food chain. This is the main weakness of using numbers rather than biomass.
7 · Pyramids of Biomass
Pyramid of biomass

Shows the total mass of organisms (dry mass or fresh mass) at each trophic level. Always (or almost always) a true pyramid shape — wider at base — because energy and biomass are lost at each trophic level.

Pyramid of biomass
(always true pyramid)
10 kg
100 kg
1000 kg
Why biomass decreases
at each level

Energy lost as:

• Heat from respiration

• Movement

• Excretion (urine/faeces)

• Uneaten parts

Only ~10% of energy passes to next level.

Energy efficiency

% efficiency = (energy out ÷ energy in) × 100

Typically only 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. This is why food chains rarely have more than 4 or 5 levels — there is too little energy left to support another level.

Shorter food chains are more energy efficient. Eating plants directly is more efficient than eating animals that ate plants.
8 · Sampling & Measuring Populations
Key sampling methods
QuadratSquare frame placed randomly in habitat. Count organisms inside. Use random number tables to place. Calculate mean per m².
TransectLine across habitat — place quadrats at intervals along line. Shows how species distribution changes across a gradient (e.g. shore, woodland edge).
Capture-recaptureCatch, mark, release animals. Recatch later. Estimate population using Lincoln Index: N = (n₁ × n₂) ÷ m
Capture-recapture formula
N = (n₁ × n₂) ÷ m
NEstimated population size
n₁Number caught and marked in first sample
n₂Total number caught in second sample
mNumber of marked individuals in second sample
Assumes: marks don't affect survival; population is closed (no immigration/emigration); marks don't come off; random mixing occurs.
Key vocabulary summary
AutotrophOrganism that makes its own food (producer). Uses photosynthesis.
HeterotrophOrganism that must consume other organisms for energy (consumer).
DecomposerBreaks down dead organic matter. Bacteria and fungi.
Trophic levelPosition in food chain. Level 1 = producer.
BiomassTotal mass of organisms at a trophic level (usually dry mass).
CompetitionStruggle for limited resources. Intra = same species; Inter = different species.
Carrying capacityMaximum population size an environment can support given available resources.
Limiting factorFactor that restricts population growth (food, space, light, water).
